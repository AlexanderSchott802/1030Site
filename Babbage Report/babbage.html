Charles Babbage (1791 - 1871)

Charles Babbage was a professor of math at Cambridge University. He became obsessed with the idea that if calculations were done mechanically they could be done with much less error. After making a small mechanical calculator in 1822 he started work on the difference engine. This was a more advanced mechanical calculator, and was discontinued in 1833 before it could finish production due to budgeting limitations.

Next Babbage started working on the analytical engine. It was very intricately designed, and had a system for a punch card input and a processor that would print the result. This is the very same computer process that defines most modern computers today, input, storage, processing, and output. Again because of funding issues this was discontinued.

As a consequence of creating the machine, he also indirectly helped create the first computer programmer, Ada Lovelace. Ada wrote programs for the analytical engine before it was completed. Much later on in 1984, long after Babbage died, the analytical engine was built with the schematics left behind, and it worked flawlessly. The success is a testament to the genius of Charles Babbage, a mind truely ahead of its time.
